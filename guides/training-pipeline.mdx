Once your eval suite is in place and your golden dataset is ready, you can kick off a training pipeline. Freesolo's pipeline runs three sequential stages — supervised fine-tuning, reinforcement learning, and prompt optimization — each building on the last. You review results at the end before anything goes to production.

<Note>
  Freesolo's team handles all GPU infrastructure across every stage of training. You never need to provision servers, configure clusters, or manage training hardware. Your job is to provide the data and review the results.
</Note>

<Steps>
  <Step title="Submit your golden dataset">
    Your golden dataset is the curated set of prompt → output pairs you assembled during the eval suite build. Each example should be labeled and verified — these are the inputs and ideal outputs the model will learn from.

    Before submitting, confirm your dataset meets these criteria:

    - **Coverage** — examples span the full range of inputs your feature handles in production, not just the easy cases
    - **Quality** — each output label has been reviewed and represents a genuinely correct, high-quality response
    - **Volume** — aim for at least 50–200 labeled examples for SFT; more is better, but quality beats quantity
    - **Format** — Freesolo accepts JSONL with `prompt` and `completion` fields; the platform UI will validate your file on upload

    Upload your dataset through the platform UI under **Training → Datasets → Upload**. The Freesolo team will review it for any quality issues before training begins.

    <Tip>
      If you're unsure whether your dataset is large or diverse enough, share it with the team before uploading. A quick review at this stage saves time later.
    </Tip>
  </Step>

  <Step title="SFT cold-start">
    Supervised fine-tuning (SFT) is the first stage of training. Freesolo fine-tunes a base model on your labeled dataset using LoRA adapters on managed GPU clusters — no infrastructure setup required on your end.

    What SFT does:

    - Adapts a general-purpose foundation model to your specific feature's domain, format, and behavior
    - Gives the model a meaningful starting point before RL begins — a model that's never seen your data makes a poor RL student
    - Produces a checkpoint that Freesolo evaluates against your eval suite to establish a baseline score

    You'll receive a baseline report showing scores across all your eval dimensions before the pipeline proceeds to RL. If the SFT baseline reveals data quality issues, Freesolo's team will flag them for you to address.

    <Info>
      LoRA (Low-Rank Adaptation) fine-tuning modifies a small number of adapter parameters rather than the full model weights. This makes training fast and cost-efficient, while keeping the base model's general capabilities intact.
    </Info>
  </Step>

  <Step title="RL training with GRPO">
    Reinforcement learning (RL) is where the model learns to maximize your feature's specific success criteria, not just imitate your labeled examples.

    Here's how it works:

    1. The SFT-trained model generates multiple candidate outputs for each input in a training batch
    2. Each candidate output is scored using your eval suite's scoring functions — hard checks, result-set checks, and LLM-as-judge quality scores
    3. Candidates are ranked by score within each batch
    4. The model updates via **GRPO** (Group Relative Policy Optimization), reinforcing behaviors that produced higher-scoring outputs and discouraging behaviors that produced lower-scoring ones
    5. This cycle repeats across thousands of training steps

    **Reward formula**

    The final score used to rank candidates combines quantity (hard, measurable signals) and quality (judgment-based signals):

    ```
    final_score = (quantity_weight × quantity_value) + (1 - quantity_weight) × quality_value

    quantity_value = tanh(profiles_above_threshold / 25)
    quality_value  = total_score / max_possible_score
    ```

    - `quantity_value` — a normalized signal derived from the number of outputs that clear your hard-check thresholds; scaled using `tanh` so that gains become harder to achieve as the model gets closer to perfect hard-check compliance
    - `quality_value` — the ratio of the output's total LLM-judge score to the maximum possible score across all rubric dimensions
    - `quantity_weight` — a tunable parameter (set between 0 and 1) that balances how much the model optimizes for hard correctness vs. perceived quality

    Freesolo's team will recommend a starting `quantity_weight` based on your feature type. You can adjust it based on how results look after the first RL run.

    <Warning>
      Setting `quantity_weight` too high causes the model to overfit to hard checks at the expense of output quality. Setting it too low causes the model to chase high quality scores while ignoring correctness constraints. Review the recommended value with the team before your first run.
    </Warning>
  </Step>

  <Step title="APO prompt optimization">
    After RL training, Freesolo runs **APO** (Automated Prompt Optimization) — a DSPy-style process that tunes the system prompt using the same scoring signals from your eval suite.

    Where RL updates the model's weights, APO updates the instructions the model receives. The two approaches are complementary: RL teaches the model *how* to perform your task; APO fine-tunes the *framing* of that task.

    APO works by:

    1. Generating candidate system prompt variants through structured mutations and perturbations
    2. Evaluating each variant against your eval suite
    3. Selecting the prompt that maximizes scores across your defined dimensions
    4. Iterating until scores plateau or a target threshold is reached

    The output is a refined system prompt that, combined with the RL-trained model weights, gives you the best possible starting configuration for production.

    <Tip>
      APO is particularly effective for improving format consistency, tone, and instruction-following. If your eval suite includes schema or format checks, APO often produces meaningful gains here even after strong RL results.
    </Tip>
  </Step>

  <Step title="Review training results">
    Before anything is deployed, Freesolo shares a full before/after comparison report. The report covers every metric tracked by your eval suite:

    | Metric | Baseline (pre-training) | Post-SFT | Post-RL | Post-APO |
    |---|---|---|---|---|
    | Execution success rate | — | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ |
    | Schema pass rate | — | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ |
    | Result-set quality | — | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ |
    | LLM-judge quality score | — | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ |
    | Average latency | — | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ |
    | Average cost per call | — | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ |
    | Token count | — | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ |

    You review this report and sign off before the trained model is made available for deployment. If any metric regresses compared to your baseline, Freesolo's team will investigate and rerun the relevant stage before proceeding.

    <Note>
      You are never required to deploy a training result you're not happy with. The review step exists specifically so you can compare results against your success criteria before making a production decision.
    </Note>
  </Step>
</Steps>
