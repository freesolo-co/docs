Deploying a trained model is not the end of the loop — it's the beginning of the Improve phase. Production monitoring gives you the signal you need to understand how your feature is actually performing, catch problems before users notice them, and decide when it's time to run another training cycle.

<Info>
  Monitoring data feeds directly back into Freesolo's Improve phase. Every metric you capture in production becomes a training signal for the next iteration, making each release better than the last.
</Info>

## What Freesolo tracks

Freesolo's monitoring layer captures the following metrics for every feature call, in real time:

<Accordion title="Per-step win rate">
  For features with multi-step pipelines, the fraction of steps where the model produced the better output in a head-to-head comparison. Tracks which steps are performing well and which are dragging down overall quality.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Execution success rate">
  The percentage of calls where the model's output executed successfully downstream — no errors, no schema violations, no failed API calls. A drop here is often the first sign of a regression.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Result-set size distribution">
  For retrieval or filtering features, the distribution of result counts returned per call. Monitors for cases where the model is generating overly narrow or overly broad queries.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Latency (p50 / p95 / p99)">
  End-to-end response time percentiles. The p95 and p99 are the most important — they tell you what your slowest users are experiencing, not just the average.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Cost per call">
  Average and 95th-percentile inference cost per invocation. Tracked against your defined cost budget to surface unexpected cost increases before they compound.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Output quality scores">
  Async LLM-as-judge scoring on a sample of production outputs, using the same rubric from your eval suite. Gives you a continuous quality signal without human review of every call.
</Accordion>

All metrics are available in the Freesolo platform UI under **Monitoring → [Feature Name]**, with time-series graphs, percentile breakdowns, and per-call trace access.

## Setting up alerts

Freesolo lets you configure threshold-based alerts for any monitored metric. When a metric crosses your defined threshold, the platform notifies you via email, Slack, or webhook.

Navigate to **Monitoring → Alerts → New Alert** to configure thresholds. Recommended starting thresholds:

| Metric | Alert condition | Suggested threshold |
|---|---|---|
| Execution success rate | Falls below | 95% |
| p95 latency | Exceeds | Your latency budget × 1.2 |
| Cost per call (p95) | Exceeds | Your cost budget × 1.25 |
| LLM-judge quality score | Falls below | Your minimum acceptable quality score |
| Result-set size: zero results | Exceeds | 5% of calls |

<Tip>
  Set your alert thresholds slightly outside your success criteria boundaries — not right at the boundary. Alerting at exactly 95% execution success means you're only notified after a problem has already materialized. Alerting at 97% gives you early warning while there's still time to investigate before it becomes user-impacting.
</Tip>

**Alert severity levels**

Freesolo supports three alert severity levels:

- **Info** — metric is drifting; worth watching but not yet urgent
- **Warning** — metric is approaching a problematic threshold; investigate soon
- **Critical** — metric has crossed an unacceptable threshold; immediate action required

Configure **Critical** alerts for execution success rate and p99 latency. Configure **Warning** alerts for cost drift and quality score trends.

## Diagnosing issues with trace logs

When an alert fires — or when you notice an unexpected pattern in your dashboards — trace logs are your first tool for root cause analysis.

Every call to your feature generates a trace that captures:

- The exact input received
- Every intermediate step in the model's pipeline
- The final output produced
- Hard check and quality check scores for that specific call
- Latency breakdown by step
- Any errors or exceptions thrown

**Finding relevant traces**

In **Monitoring → Traces**, filter by:

- Time range (narrow to when the problem started)
- Execution success = false (to find failed calls)
- Quality score below your threshold (to find low-quality outputs)
- Latency above a threshold (to find slow calls)

**Reading a trace**

Each trace shows the pipeline as a sequence of steps. Look for:

- Steps where the model's output changed unexpectedly
- Steps with disproportionately high latency
- Error messages from downstream systems (the query executor, the API, the schema validator)
- Inputs that are unusual in format, length, or content compared to your training data

<Note>
  If a trace doesn't give you enough context to diagnose the issue, share the trace ID with Freesolo's engineering team. They can pull additional model-level diagnostics that aren't exposed in the platform UI.
</Note>

**Using error logs**

In addition to per-call traces, Freesolo aggregates error logs by error type and frequency. Navigate to **Monitoring → Error Logs** to see:

- The most common error types in a given time window
- Example inputs that triggered each error type
- The frequency trend (is it getting more or less common?)

Error log patterns often reveal systematic issues: a class of inputs the model was never trained on, a downstream API that changed its schema, or a rate limit being hit under load.

## Triggering a re-training cycle

Monitoring doesn't just tell you when something is broken — it tells you when the model can be meaningfully improved. These are the signals that suggest it's time to run a new training pipeline:

<Steps>
  <Step title="Quality score degradation">
    If your LLM-as-judge quality score drops by more than 5–10% from your post-deployment baseline, it's a signal that production inputs have drifted away from your training distribution. Collect recent low-scoring traces, add them to your dataset as labeled examples, and kick off a new training run.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Execution success rate decline">
    A sustained drop in execution success rate — even a few percentage points — often indicates a change in the downstream system your feature integrates with (schema changes, API updates, new data patterns). Identify the failing cases through error logs, update your eval suite to cover them, and retrain.
  </Step>
  <Step title="New failure modes appearing in production">
    If you discover a failure mode in production that wasn't in your eval suite, add it immediately as a regression test and schedule a retraining cycle. Don't wait for metrics to deteriorate significantly — capturing new failure modes early keeps your eval suite current and prevents future regressions.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Periodic improvement cycles">
    Even when nothing is broken, regular retraining on accumulated production data tends to compound quality improvements over time. Freesolo recommends reviewing monitoring data monthly and running a new RL pass whenever you've accumulated enough new labeled examples to meaningfully expand your training set.
  </Step>
</Steps>
