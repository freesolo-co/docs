Evaluation is where intention meets evidence. You can have strong intuitions about what makes your AI feature good, but without a systematic, reproducible way to measure it, those intuitions don't translate into reliable improvement. Freesolo's evaluation phase is designed around a single principle: measure what your product actually requires, not what is convenient to measure. That means moving past accuracy scores on abstract benchmarks and building evaluation pipelines that reflect the real success criteria of your feature — the ones your users experience every time they interact with it.

## Freesolo's Evaluation Philosophy

Generic benchmarks measure what a model can do in the best case, on inputs it was tested on, in isolation from the systems around it. Your product needs to know what the model does in your case — with your prompts, your data, your downstream schema, and your latency budget. These are not the same measurement.

Freesolo grounds evaluation in the instrumented behavioral data captured during the Instrument phase. Evaluation sets are built from real user queries, clustered to ensure representative coverage across input types, and scored against your feature's specific success criteria. The result is an evaluation signal that is tightly coupled to production behavior and directly actionable — a score drop tells you something real degraded, and a score improvement tells you something real got better.

## The Five Evaluation Dimensions

Freesolo evaluates every model and prompt configuration across five dimensions simultaneously. No single dimension tells the full story; trade-offs between them are often where the most important decisions live.

## Hard Checks: Non-Negotiable Quality Gates

Before any LLM-as-judge scoring runs, Freesolo applies a set of hard, deterministic checks that a model output must pass. These checks are binary — pass or fail — and they catch the failure modes that are most damaging to product experience.

**Execution success** verifies that the model's output was processable by the downstream system. If the feature expects the model to generate a SQL query, a structured JSON payload, or a function call, execution success checks that the output was syntactically and semantically valid enough to actually run.

**Schema compliance** checks that the output conforms to the expected structure — the right fields, the right types, the right format. Schema failures are silent bugs in production: the model returns something, but the downstream system can't use it.

**Result-set size** catches two opposite failure modes that are easy to miss with aggregate metrics. A result set that is too broad means the model returned too many results with insufficient specificity — useful signal is buried in noise. A result set that is too narrow — or empty — means the model failed to surface relevant information at all. Both are failures. Freesolo measures the distribution of result-set sizes, not just whether results were returned.

## LLM-as-Judge Scoring

Hard checks tell you whether an output is structurally usable. They don't tell you whether it's good. For non-deterministic quality dimensions — coherence, relevance, helpfulness, accuracy — Freesolo uses LLM-as-judge scoring: a separate, calibrated model evaluates each output against a defined rubric and assigns a score.

LLM-as-judge scales evaluation in a way that human review cannot. For any meaningful volume of instrumented traces, manual scoring is a bottleneck; automated judge scoring runs continuously alongside your feature's live traffic. The judge model is calibrated against your product's standards during the Instrument phase, ensuring that its scores reflect your definition of quality — not a generic one.

Judge scores are tracked per-step across your model pipeline, so you can identify which step in a multi-step feature is responsible for quality degradation. This step-level visibility is essential for targeted improvement: if step three is failing, you fix step three.

<Tip>
Evaluation benefits from a fundamental asymmetry: it is much easier to verify whether a result is good than it is to produce a good result in the first place. A judge model checking an output for schema compliance, relevance, and coherence is solving a simpler problem than the model that produced the output — and that means automated evaluation can run at scale and still be reliable. This asymmetry is why LLM-as-judge evaluation works: you don't need the judge to be perfect, you need it to be consistently calibrated. Use your hard-check pass rates and a sample of human-reviewed traces to verify calibration periodically, and your automated eval pipeline will remain trustworthy as your feature and your data evolve.
</Tip>

## Evaluation Sets: From Raw Queries to Representative Samples

Building an evaluation set by hand — picking a few test cases that seem representative — introduces selection bias. The cases you pick consciously tend to be the ones you already know about, which means your evaluation set systematically under-samples the edge cases and failure modes that are hardest for your model.

Freesolo builds evaluation sets by embedding your instrumented customer queries and clustering them to find natural groupings in the input space. Representative samples are drawn from each cluster, ensuring that your evaluation set covers the full distribution of real user inputs — including the uncommon patterns that are often responsible for the most visible failures.

## Tracking Progression: SFT → RL → APO

Freesolo's evaluation pipeline is designed to track improvement across the full training lifecycle, not just at a single snapshot. As your model moves through Supervised Fine-Tuning, Reinforcement Learning, and Automatic Prompt Optimization, the same evaluation dimensions and the same representative test sets are used at each stage.

This consistency is intentional. It means that a score improvement at the RL stage is directly comparable to the SFT baseline — you can see exactly how much each intervention contributed. Per-step win rate, execution success rate, and result-set size histograms are tracked continuously, giving you a clear progression curve from cold-start to optimized deployment.
