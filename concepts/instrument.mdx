Before you can improve a model's behavior in your product, you need an honest record of what that behavior actually looks like. Instrumentation is Freesolo's first phase, and its purpose is precise: capture the real workflow, the real user task, and every point where model behavior either helps the experience or breaks it. Without that grounding, evaluation scores are abstract and improvement is directionally unreliable. With it, every subsequent phase operates on evidence rather than assumption.

## What Instrumentation Means in Freesolo

Instrumentation in Freesolo is not generic logging. It is structured observation of a specific product feature — the inputs the model receives, the decisions it makes, the outputs it returns, and the context that surrounds all of it. The goal is to produce a behavioral record that is rich enough to support evaluation and targeted enough to be actionable.

This means capturing data at every meaningful step in the feature's model pipeline, not just at the final output boundary. If your feature involves multi-step reasoning, tool use, or retrieval, Freesolo instruments each step independently so you can identify exactly where quality breaks down — not just that it does.

## What Gets Captured

<Steps>
  <Step title="Prompts and System Context">
    Every prompt sent to the model — including system instructions, few-shot examples, and injected context — is recorded verbatim. This gives you a reproducible snapshot of the model's input at the moment of inference, which is essential for evaluation and for detecting prompt drift over time.
  </Step>
  <Step title="User Inputs and Feature Context">
    The raw user input that triggered the feature, along with any feature-level context passed alongside it (session state, user preferences, retrieved documents, schema constraints), is captured as a first-class artifact. This is the signal that connects model behavior to actual user intent.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Model Outputs">
    The full model response — not truncated, not post-processed — is stored alongside any structured outputs your feature extracts from it. When outputs are compared across model versions during evaluation, this record ensures you are comparing apples to apples.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Latency Traces">
    Wall-clock latency is measured at the step level, not just end-to-end. Freesolo tracks time-to-first-token, generation time, and any downstream processing time separately, giving you a decomposed view of where latency is actually incurred.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Error Logs and Failure Signals">
    Execution failures, schema violations, empty result sets, and timeout events are all captured and tagged. These failure signals become the hardest and most valuable evaluation inputs — they represent the exact conditions your model must learn to handle.
  </Step>
</Steps>

## Why Starting With the Product Feature Matters

The alternative to product-grounded instrumentation is benchmark-driven evaluation — running your model against a static test set that was designed to measure general capability. Benchmarks are useful for comparing models in the abstract, but they tell you almost nothing about how a model will behave under your system prompt, with your retrieval context, against your schema, with your users' phrasing patterns.

Instrumentation flips the direction of measurement. Instead of asking "how does this model perform on the benchmark?", Freesolo asks "how does this model perform on the actual inputs your feature receives?" That shift produces evaluation data that is directly actionable — you know exactly which inputs cause failures, which prompts are fragile, and which user patterns the model handles poorly.

It also means your evaluation sets are built from real user behavior, not synthetic queries. When Freesolo clusters your instrumented queries to build representative evaluation samples, it is clustering things your users actually asked — not things a researcher imagined they might ask.

## How Instrumentation Feeds the Engine

Instrumented data flows forward into both evaluation and improvement:

- **Evaluation** uses instrumented traces to construct hard-check test cases (did the model return a schema-valid result? did it return any results at all?) and to seed the LLM-as-judge scoring pipeline with real input/output pairs.
- **Improvement** uses failure traces to identify where Supervised Fine-Tuning labels should be concentrated, and uses win/loss signals from live traffic to compute reward signals for Reinforcement Learning.

The richer and more complete your instrumentation, the more precisely the rest of the Engine can target its work. Instrumentation is not overhead — it is the data foundation that makes every downstream phase faster and more accurate.

<Note>
Capture edge cases and failure modes deliberately, not just happy paths. A perfectly instrumented success trace tells you what works. A well-instrumented failure tells you what to fix. In practice, the most valuable improvement signal comes from the inputs your model currently handles worst — the near-misses, the empty results, the schema violations, and the cases where the model produced a plausible-sounding but incorrect output. If your instrumentation only covers nominal flows, your evaluation sets will be systematically optimistic and your improvement cycles will under-target the inputs that matter most to your users.
</Note>
